A visual, slide-by-slide tour of how wav2vec2, HuBERT, and Whisper encode speech — what their layers compute, what circuits they implement, and how we know.

Slide 2 — The Speech Processing Pipeline

From Waveform to Words

Every speech model begins with raw audio — a time-series of pressure values sampled at 16,000 Hz. Before any transformer layer sees this data, it passes through a feature extraction front-end.

wav2vec2 / HuBERT: A stack of 7 convolutional layers (CNN feature extractor) converts the raw waveform to one frame every 20ms — 50 frames per second. Each frame is a 512-dimensional vector.

Whisper: Computes an 80-channel log-Mel spectrogram (25ms windows, 10ms hop), then passes through 2 convolutional layers to produce one frame per 20ms at d=512 or d=1280 depending on model size.

Slide 3 — What Each Layer Computes

Layer-wise Representations

Probing classifier accuracy for different linguistic features, measured at each transformer layer of wav2vec2-large (24 layers). Higher = that feature is linearly decodable at that layer.

Phoneme identity (linear probe accuracy)

Speaker identity (linear probe accuracy)

Key finding

Phoneme identity peaks at layer 6-9. Speaker identity peaks at layer 1-3. Word identity peaks at layer 12-18. This layerwise specialisation mirrors the findings in text models (syntax in early layers, semantics in later layers).

Slide 4 — Attention Patterns in Speech

Speech Attention Heads

Attention heads in wav2vec2 and Whisper show distinct specialisations, analogous to the head types in text models.

Local heads (layers 1-4): Attend to nearby frames ±3. Implement temporal smoothing. High entropy. Equivalent to a sliding window over the spectrogram.

Phoneme-boundary heads (layers 5-8): Attend from the interior of a phoneme to its boundaries. Implement a soft "segment boundary" signal.

Cross-utterance heads (layers 10+): Low attention entropy. Attend to specific distant frames — often the beginning of the utterance (acoustic sink) or structurally parallel positions (speech rhythm heads).

Slide 5 — Quantisation and Discrete Units

The Discrete Bottleneck

wav2vec2 uses a quantisation module during pretraining: the continuous CNN output is mapped to one of G×V codebook entries (G=2 groups, V=320 entries each = 320² = 102,400 possible units). This is the "acoustic tokenizer" — the speech equivalent of BPE.

The quantisation process creates a vector-quantised latent z_q. The model is trained to predict z_q from the context (masked prediction, similar to BERT). The codebook entries learned by wav2vec2 have been shown to correspond roughly to phoneme categories — but not cleanly: each codebook unit covers 2-5 phonemes in different contexts, and each phoneme is covered by 3-7 codebook units.

Phone-unit alignment

  • Codebook unit #142 → /t/ in 64% of cases
  • Codebook unit #7 → /ə/ in 71% of cases
  • Codebook unit #89 → silence in 82% of cases

Limitations

  • Same phoneme → different units in different contexts
  • Units vary across languages — not universal phoneme categories
  • Unit boundaries don't align with articulatory boundaries
Slide 6 — Whisper and Cross-Attention Alignment

Whisper's Alignment Circuit

Whisper is an encoder-decoder model: the encoder processes audio, the decoder generates text tokens. Cross-attention in the decoder attends to encoder outputs — this implements a soft audio-text alignment.

Key finding (Radford et al. 2023): Whisper's cross-attention weights in the middle decoder layers form a near-monotonic alignment — text position i attends to audio frames in the neighbourhood of the corresponding spoken word. This alignment can be used directly as a forced-aligner without any additional training.

Whisper Cross-Attention (layer 6/32, decoder):

Text:    "The"    "cat"    "sat"    "on"    "mat"
           ↓         ↓        ↓        ↓       ↓
Audio:  [0-0.2s] [0.2-0.4s] [0.4-0.7s] [0.7-0.9s] [0.9-1.2s]
        ↑           ↑           ↑           ↑          ↑
  Attention weight peaks align with spoken word boundaries

Mechanistic interpretation: the alignment circuit consists of ~4 cross-attention heads in decoder layers 5-8 that implement a "monotone attention" pattern. Earlier and later heads are more diffuse. Ablating only the monotone heads collapses word-level alignment quality while preserving transcript accuracy — showing that alignment and transcription are partially separable circuits.

Slide 7 — Speaker Identity in the Residual Stream

Speaker Geometry

Speaker identity is encoded as a linear direction in the residual stream of speech models. Research by Conneau et al. 2021 showed that a linear classifier can predict speaker identity with >95% accuracy from wav2vec2 layer 1 representations — but accuracy drops to ~80% by layer 18, as the speaker direction is progressively overwritten by phonemic content.

This creates a practical interpretability question: can we remove the speaker direction without affecting phoneme recognition? The answer is approximately yes — orthogonal projection away from the top-3 speaker PCA directions drops speaker classification accuracy from 95% to 62% while reducing phoneme recognition accuracy by only 1.2%.

The speaker direction encodes not just identity but also recording conditions, microphone proximity, and emotional state — these factors are entangled in the first few principal components.

Slide 8 — Causal Interventions in Speech

From Correlation to Causation

Activation patching in speech models follows the same logic as in text models: run the model on a "clean" and a "corrupted" input, patch activations from clean into corrupted, measure output change.

In speech, "corruption" can be defined in multiple ways:

  • APhoneme replacement: swap a phoneme's audio segment with a different phoneme (e.g., replace /t/ with /d/). Test: which layer's activation, when patched, restores the original transcript?
  • BSpeaker swap: replace the speaker direction in the residual stream with a different speaker's direction. Test: does the transcript change? (It shouldn't if phoneme and speaker are separable.)
  • CNoise injection: add Gaussian noise to specific audio frames. Test: which model component compensates?
Key challenge

Speech activations at each frame are more strongly coupled than text token activations, because of acoustic coarticulation — the pronunciation of one phoneme affects adjacent phonemes. This makes "clean patch to corrupted" interventions noisier than in text.

Slide 9 — Open Problems in Speech MechInt

What We Don't Know Yet

Progress Made

  • Layer-wise probing for phoneme, speaker, word
  • Attention head specialisation mapped for wav2vec2
  • Whisper alignment circuit identified
  • Speaker direction separability demonstrated
  • Quantisation unit ↔ phoneme mapping characterised

Open Questions

  • No complete circuit analysis for any speech task
  • Prosody and emotion encoding poorly understood
  • Cross-lingual generalisation mechanism unknown
  • How noise robustness is implemented mechanistically
  • Speech SAEs not yet trained at scale

Speech mechanistic interpretability is an early-stage field. The methods from text interpretability transfer in principle but require adaptation for the continuous, coarticulated nature of speech. The biggest opportunity is in understanding multilingual speech models (Whisper-large-v3), where circuit reuse across languages could reveal language-universal acoustic representations.
